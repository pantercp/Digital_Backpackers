Colombia

Ever since their discovery in the 16th century, the Galapagos have held fascination for travellers far and wide. But what exactly makes the islands so special? Completely isolated from the outside world, a new wave of fearless endemic species were freely left to evolve. In fact, this bubble is so unique that it inspired the basis for Darwin’s theory of evolution.

Things To Know

So, first question to ask yourself when considering a trip to the Galapagos? Essentially, it’s choosing how to visit. The two main options are cruises (often very expensive), or staying in accommodation on land (more budget-friendly).

Culture

The Galapagos Islands are home to an abundance of wildlife due to their unique geographical location and isolation from the mainland. The islands are located in the Pacific Ocean, over 900 kilometers off the coast of Ecuador. This isolation has allowed species to evolve independently, leading to the development of unique adaptations and characteristics.

Additionally, the islands' diverse habitats, including volcanic terrain, beaches, and mangroves, provide a range of niches for different species to thrive. Furthermore, the Galapagos National Park has implemented strict conservation measures to protect the islands' wildlife and their habitats, which has contributed to the preservation of the unique and diverse ecosystems found in the Galapagos.

Our Trip

We spent an unforgettable 14 days in the Galapagos last year staying on all three of the main islands. We have shared our itinerary, expenses, excursions and more all so that you may plan a successful trip.


Two-Week Itinerary

With so much to see and do in this unique corner of the world, making the most out of your time can become overwhelming. This is why we have shared our two week itinerary for your reference:

Day 1 - 5

Santa Cruz

▪️ DAY 1 – Fly into Santa Cruz and get settled at your hostel. Find a good bakery for breakfasts, and scout the many agencies to gauge the cost of tours. Walk to the fish market to spot your first sea lions, marine iguanas and pelicans. Keep walking to Playa de la Estacion to snorkel or lounge.

▪️ DAY 2 – Make the 40-minute walk to Tortuga Bay in the morning – one of the world’s most beautiful beaches. Continue onto Playa Mansa for snorkelling. In the afternoon, take a water taxi across to Las Grietas for snorkelling between these epic rock formations.

▪️ DAY 3 – Full-day trip to Isla Bartolome – the quintessential Galapagos island. After a 2.5 hour boat journey, you’ll begin by hiking to the summit for that most iconic view. Swimming with sea lions, sharks and possibly even Galapagos penguins comes next, followed by lunch and a 2.5 hour ride back to Santa Cruz.

▪️ DAY 4 – Visit the Charles Darwin Research Station to learn about the islands’ history and biodiversity. The site also houses a tortoise rehabilitation centre. End the day at one of the beaches or Lagunas de las Ninfas.

▪️ DAY 5 – Rent bikes or take a taxi to El Chato Ranch to see semi-wild giant tortoises and the remarkable Bellavista lava tunnels. Los Gemelos are also closeby, two collapsed volcano calderas that have been reclaimed by nature. Enjoy your final evening in Santa Cruz.

Day 6 - 9

Isla Isabela

▪️ DAY 6 – Take the ferry from Santa Cruz to Isla Isabela. Once you’ve freshed up at your hostel, go find a bakery, a merienda dinner menu for later and enquire about tour prices. Spend the rest of the day walking along La Playita, or spotting flamingos at Laguna Salinas.

▪️ DAY 7 – Start the day bright and early with a tour to Los Tuneles, where you will make several stops to snorkel and explore the otherworldly landscapes. Expect to see sharks, sea turtles, penguins, sea horses and various rays.

▪️ DAY 8 – Rent a bike or hike to The Wall of Tears, the Galapagos’ most haunting historic site. Along the way, you’ll pass several smaller beaches and paths leading to viewpoints, lakes and natural coves. Keep a look out for wild giant tortoises on this route too – we saw five in total!

▪️ DAY 9 – Join a tour and trek up Sierra Negra, home to the world’s second largest volcanic crater. Continue onto the lunar-like landscapes of Cerro Azul. Then, spend the afternoon snorkelling at Concha de Perla, before a relaxing final evening on Isabela.

Day 10 - 14

San Cristobal

▪️ DAY 10 – Take the morning ferry back to Santa Cruz. Kill time at the beach or camp out in a cafe, before catching the afternoon ferry to San Cristobal. Be sure to pack motion sickness tablets for this long-winded travel day.

Arrive on San Cristobal after a long travel day. Take in the many sea lions lounging by the docks. If you check-in and drop your bags off in time for sunset, then head on over to Playa Mann. Then, scout the side roads of Puerto Baquerizo Moreno for your first merienda here.

▪️ DAY 11 – Begin with a visit to the Interpretation Center, possibly the most informative exhibition on the Galapagos. Head up to Cerro Tijeretas next to get your fill of island vistas, followed by snorkelling at Las Tijeretas, a spot frequented by sea turtles, sea lions and even sharks.

▪️ DAY 12 – Embark on the 360 Tour, where you will sail around the whole of San Cristobal, making several beach and snorkel stops. The main highlight comes at the end – Kicker Rock – a vertical formation jutting wildly out of the ocean, with some of the Galapagos’ best diving found below. Prepare to see all kinds of wildlife, including hammerhead sharks if you’re lucky.

▪️ DAY 13 – Your final full day on the Galapagos is reserved for La Loberia. It’s an easy 2km walk from town. Eventually, you’ll reach a sheltered cove with great snorkelling. Continue onto El Barranco for a chance to see blue-footed boobies. Then, enjoy one final sunset at Playa Mann.

▪️ DAY 14 – Depending on when your flight is, you may have time for a final stroll through town before heading to the airport. If you plan to buy gifts or souvenirs, then this is a final chance to browse through some shops.

Food & Accommodation

We did however manage to keep our food & accommodation costs pretty low, on average spending between $4-7pp per dinner out. We stayed in hostels on each island, which averaged out to £13pp per night over 13 nights.

Flights & Taxes

The two biggest costs were flights & excursions, alongside the $100 tourist tax you must pay upon arrival. Therefore, while flights are generally around this price year-round, you can choose to spend less depending on the excursions you do.

Excursions

Initially, we budgeted $100 each per island for tours ($300 total each). As you can see, we exceeded this, but it was a price we were willing to pay for the incredible experiences that followed. The three tours we did do were Isla Bartolome ($200pp), Los Tuneles ($90pp) and the 360 Tour on San Cristobal ($160pp). More to come soon on each experience!

Thoughts

This is (and probably will be for a while) our most expensive breakdown to date, especially when compared to our usual daily budget of £25 each. However, opportunities don't come by often to visit this unworldly paradise, so we believe it was something well worth stretching the budget for.

Preperation For The Galapagos

There’s a pre-registration form to complete before flying into the Galapagos. It’s similar to COVID entry forms, in that it asks where and for how long you’re staying on the islands. Be sure to fill this out ahead of your trip through this link.

All visitors must purchase a tourist transit card at the airport in mainland Ecuador. This costs $20 and is something you need to keep as it has to be returned upon your departure. It is separate to the $100 entry tax visitors pay upon arrival. However, if you are Ecuadorian, or hold nationality from another South American country, this tax is cheaper.

November to May is the best time to go, when the weather is warmer and the oceans are most calm and prime for snorkelling/diving. Our 2-week trip was in June, during which we had a mix of cloudy skies and sun, as well as some very choppy ferry crossings.

Budgeting

Being a remote archipaelego and a place of focused conservation efforts (rightly so), The Galapagos Islands is never going to be one of your cheapest visits. However, with careful planning and a bit of research we believe you can stretch your budget to get the most value. Here are some of our best tips on how we managed to save a significant amount of money:

Budgeting Tips

ATMs

Cash is king in the Galapagos and not many places take card, so be sure to withdraw as much as you’re comfortable carrying before flying out. There are ATMs on the islands, but almost all have a fee (typically $4) as well as a withdrawal limit per day ($200-$300). Note: Isla Isabela is most limited so be sure to withdraw before travelling here. Supposedly there is only one ATM on the whole island, but even that was out of order at the time of our visit.

Meriendas

The golden rule for eating cheap on the Galapagos: look for meriendas. These are set dinner menus offered at local restaurants, and typically come with a starter and drink too. Our most expensive dinner was $7 each, our cheapest was $4, so this really is a great way to get value for money compared to touristy restaurants that could see you spending $20 per meal.

Ferries

There are only two crossings between the islands per day, so if you have set travel dates, don’t delay booking this. A one-way ferry typically costs $30 per person, however some agencies may offer a small discount if you book a return journey. We got a discounted rate for booking all journeys with our hostel in Santa Cruz.

Tours

This is where haggling can really pay off, but some alternative strategies for finding cheap tours include being flexible. Most agencies offer last-minute rates for tours leaving the next morning, so make sure to enquire about this first. You could also bag a discount by booking all your tours with one agency, or liaisoning with other travellers and booking in a group.

Final note: if there’s a tour you really have your heart set on, be sure to secure this first, as some tours to more remote islands e.g. Isla Bartolome can sell out days or even weeks in advance.
